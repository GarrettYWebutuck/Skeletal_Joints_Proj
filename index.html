Skeletal Joints

What are Joints?

Joints are the places where two or more bones meet. They are held together in a way that stops them from falling apart but also in a way that they can be moved. Joints are also reffered to as 'articulations'. Joints can be classified into two ways: Functional and Structural. From here on we will focus on the structural classification.

Types of Joints

When using the structural classification of joints, there are three main subclassifications: Fibrous, Cartilaginous, and Synovial. These subclassifications are based on what separates the bones at a joint.

Structural Subclassifications

  • Fibrous Joints

    In fibrous joints, the bones are joined by fibrous tissue. The types of fibrous joints are called "Sutures", "Syndesmoses", and "Gomphoses". The places you would find these joints are in the seams of the skull growth plates to hold the skull together(Suture Joints), in the ankle to hold the tibia and fibula together(Syndesmoses Joints), and in the mouth to hold the teeth to the facial bones (Gomphoses Joints).

  • Cartilaginous Joints

    Cartilaginous joints can have a little movement or no movement at all, it depends on the type of cartilage that joins the bones. The types of cartilaginous joints are called "Synchrondroses", and "Symphyses". Synchrondroses use hyaline cartilage to hold the bones together and are immovable. Places you will find this type of cartilaginous joint are the connection between the ribs and the sternum. Symphyses use disks of fibrocartilage to hold bones together and are slightly moveable. One of the places you will find this type of cartilaginous joint are the intervertebral disks in the spine.

  • Synovial Joints

    Synovial joints can move freely. Every joint in your limbs are synovial joints. The bones in synovial joints are separated by a joint cavity that contains synovial fluid. Every synovial joint has four unique features.

    1. Articular (hyaline) cartilage covers the ends of the bones at the joint.

    2. The joint surface is encasulated by an articular capsule made of fibrous tissue that is lined by a synovial membrane.

    3. The inside of the capsule is called the joint cavity and is filled with synovial fluid that is secreted by the synovial membrane.

    4. The fibrous tissue in the synovial membrane is usually reinforced by ligaments.

    There are many types of synovial joints and they will be discussed in the next section.

Types of Synovial Joints

The movements of synovial joints are determined by the shape of the bone in the joint.

  • Plane Joints

    The bones in these joints glide across each other. The joint is considered nonaxial because it doesnt rotate on any defined axis. The best example of this synovial joint are the carpal bones in the wrist.

  • Hinge Joints

    The bones in these joints move like a hinge. The joint is considered uniaxial because it rotates on only one defined axis. The best examples o fthis synovial joint are the knee, elbow, ankle, and phalanges joints.

  • Pivot Joints

    The bones in these joints rotate on their long axis. The joint is considered uniaxial because it rotates on only one defined axis. The best example of this joint is the pivot joint on your elbow that allows you to flip your hand over.

  • Condylar Joints

    The bones in these joints are connected by a sphere of bone in the middle that is part of one of the bones in the joint. This joint is considered biaxial because it allows for rotation on two defined axes. The best example of these joints is the joint that connects the finger to the palm.

  • Saddle Joints

    The bones in these joints are connected in the shape of a saddle. The joint is considered biaxial because it allows for rotation in two axes. The best exampe of this joint is the joint that connects the thumb to the wrist/palm.

  • Ball-And-Socket Joints

    These joints are connected by the spherical end of one of the bones in the spherical cavity of the other bone. These joints are considered multiaxial because they allow for rotation on more than two axes. The best examples of this joint are the shoulders and hips.

Fun Facts

These are some fun facts about skeletal joints.

  1. There is only one bone in the body that isnt part of a joint, called the Hyoid Bone. It is located in the neck and holds the tongue as well as translates some muscle movements to the jaw.
